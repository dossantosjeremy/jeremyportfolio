# Designing AI That Feels Alive: Research for Renault's Next-Generation Vehicle Intelligence

---

## The Context

Renault was developing GEN1 and GEN3 vehicle platforms with AI integrated into the driving experience — not just as a voice assistant, but as a system that could feel, in some sense, alive. The design challenge: which "living" characteristics could build genuine user attachment to the vehicle? And where does that cross the line into something that feels intrusive or wrong?

No validated framework existed for measuring this. I built one.

I led three interconnected research phases across the GEN1 and GEN3 platforms over three years. The findings fed directly into the Renault/Nissan/Mitsubishi Alliance AI development roadmap.

---

## The Problem

There's a well-known tension in AI design: users want systems that feel intuitive and warm, but they resist systems that feel intrusive or unpredictable. In automotive contexts, this is sharpened — a car AI that behaves unexpectedly isn't just annoying, it's a safety concern.

When we ran preliminary association work, the top spontaneous associations with in-car AI were "computing" and "algorithms" (14% each). But "life facilitation" came third at 10%. Users could see the value — they also had specific anxieties about unwanted AI behaviours.

Renault needed to know: which features would users accept? Which living characteristics should be prioritised? Where are the risks? Without research to answer these questions, engineering teams would build features based on assumptions.

---

## What I Did

### Phase 1 — Building the framework and testing it (GEN1)

Before any user research ran, I constructed a theoretical framework for what "attachment to AI" could mean in a vehicle context. Drawing from HCI literature and attachment theory, I identified five factors:

1. AI Emotion — affective connection
2. AI Adaptation — responsiveness to user preferences
3. User Control of AI Relationship — sense of agency
4. AI Memory — persistence and learning
5. Context Sharing — situationally aware communication

These became the measurement backbone.

I then developed 20 potential use cases, refined them through expert review to 12, and selected 5 for testing:
1. Vehicle approach and recognition (Firefly welcome sequence)
2. Ambient cabin lighting (guided search assistance)
3. Pre-drive check (readiness feedback)
4. Dynamic driving response (adaptive feedback)
5. Intelligent exit assistance (safe egress detection)

These were presented as storyboards to 29 Renault employees in an online survey. Each scenario was rated across attractiveness, utility, ease of use, pleasure, and perceived living characteristics.

I chose storyboard evaluation specifically because the technology was still conceptual. Storyboards let users react to ideas without the noise of technical implementation — you get signal on the concept, not on prototyping quality.

### Phase 2 — Physical design expert review (GEN1 V2)

Based on Phase 1 findings, a physical design was developed implementing the "Firefly" metaphor — subtle, glowing light behaviours inspired by bioluminescence. Expert review sessions assessed usability, safety implications, and alignment with the attachment model.

### Phase 3 — Emotion by AI (GEN3)

Extended the research to the GEN3 platform, focusing on how AI could express and respond to emotional states — building on the validated attachment model from Phases 1 and 2.

## Key Findings

**Utility drives adoption — not attachment.**

Regression analysis found utility as the primary predictor of feature acceptance (β=0.49, p<0.0001). Attractiveness was a significant amplifier (β=0.27, p<0.01) but not a driver on its own. Users who found a feature genuinely useful were significantly more willing to accept its "alive" characteristics than users who saw it as interesting but not useful.

**Scenario ranking:**

| Scenario | Reception | Key factor |
|---|---|---|
| S5 — Intelligent exit | Highest acceptance | Utility clear and obvious |
| S1 — Approach & recognition | Moderate | Attractiveness, ease |
| S2 — Ambient lighting | Moderate | Attractiveness, ease |
| S3 — Pre-drive check | Lower | Mixed utility/attachment signal |
| S4 — Dynamic driving response | Lower | Living traits felt intrusive |

**The Firefly metaphor worked.** Across all five scenarios, the subtle light-based embodiment was consistently perceived as non-threatening and contextually appropriate. It became the reference visual metaphor for the GEN1 V2 physical design.

**Design principles for the Alliance:**
1. Utility first — no feature without a clear functional benefit
2. User control is required, not optional — customisation and override capability are acceptance-critical
3. Context matters — the same AI behaviour lands differently in urban vs. rural, experienced vs. novice contexts
4. Progressive disclosure — introduce living characteristics gradually, not all at once
5. Safety and reliability before everything — clear system status, fail-safe mechanisms

---

## The Impact

- First validated framework for measuring user attachment to AI in automotive contexts
- Utility identified as the primary adoption driver — deprioritised features with low utility scores before design investment
- Firefly metaphor advanced from research concept to physical prototype
- Design principles codified as internal Alliance standards
- Research that started as one internship project evolved into a three-year programme across GEN1 and GEN3

---

## What I Learned

The combination of rigorous theory and storyboard-based evaluation was the right approach for emerging technology research. When the technology doesn't exist yet, you can't test implementation quality. You're testing whether the concept makes sense to users — and storyboards are the cleanest way to do that.

The participant pool (Renault employees) skewed toward technology-literate users. That's a validity limitation I documented. Consumer-facing generalisation requires broader sampling — ideally external participants across multiple markets.

The open question I'd most want to answer next: do the acceptance patterns hold across cultures? The Alliance spans France, Japan, and multiple emerging markets. The European findings may not transfer directly.
