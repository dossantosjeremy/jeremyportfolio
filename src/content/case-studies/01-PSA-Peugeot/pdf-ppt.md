# PSA Peugeot Citroën — Neural Up® Driver Relaxation Study
### Slide / PDF Deck Version

## SLIDE 1 — Hook

**Can sound measurably reduce driver stress?**

I designed and ran PSA's first empirical validation of an in-car auditory relaxation technology — discovering that *consistency* of sound, not richness or complexity, is what makes drivers relax.

## SLIDE 3 — The Problem

**The challenge:** Drivers accumulate stress during long journeys. Short breaks don't reset arousal without a deliberate intervention.

**The technology:** Neural Up® — a rotational auditory stimulus — proposed to reduce driver stress during pauses. No automotive validation existed.

**PSA's question:** Does it actually work? And which version is safe to integrate?

> *"Drivers need a way to decompress during breaks — but no one had tested whether Neural Up® could actually do that."*

---

## SLIDE 4 — My Role

I owned the entire research lifecycle:
- Experimental design and counterbalancing strategy
- Participant recruitment (75 PSA employees)
- Physiological data collection (EDA + HR) across all sessions
- Psychometric questionnaire adaptation and administration
- Statistical analysis (descriptive stats, PCA, regression)
- Findings report and R&D stakeholder presentation

---

## SLIDE 5 — Approach

**Three conditions, tested within-subjects (randomized order):**

| Condition | Description |
|---|---|
| Stationary | Sound without rotation |
| Consistent rotation | Fixed, predictable rotational speed |
| Variable rotation | Changing, unpredictable speeds |

**Study flow per session:**
1. Baseline mood questionnaire
2. 10-min simulated driving (to establish context)
3. 9-min sound exposure (one condition)
4. Post-exposure questionnaire + continuous EDA & HR monitoring

**Key methodological decision:** Dual-measure design (physiological + self-report) to eliminate demand characteristics from self-report alone.

---

## SLIDE 6 — Key Findings

| Condition | Physiological Effect | Self-Reported |
|---|---|---|
| Stationary | No significant change | Neutral |
| **Consistent rotation** | **−20% arousal (EDA)** | **85% relaxation** |
| Variable rotation | No benefit | 60% discomfort / nausea |

> *"The rotating sound felt calming and helped me relax during the break."*
> *"The changing speed made me feel slightly dizzy and uncomfortable."*

**The non-obvious insight:** Stationary sound — assumed to be at least mildly calming — had effectively zero relaxation effect. Active rotation, not passive presence, drives the response.

---

## SLIDE 7 — Impact

**Immediate decisions enabled:**
- Variable-speed rotation **removed from product roadmap** before any engineering investment
- Consistent rotation **validated as viable relaxation feature** — advancing to next phase
- PSA adopted dual-measure framework as standard for all future in-car sensory evaluations

**Quantitative impact:**
- 20% arousal reduction (consistent rotation vs. baseline)
- 85% participant relaxation rate
- Methodology replicated in subsequent in-car wellness studies

---

## SLIDE 8 — Learnings

**What I'd repeat:** The dual physiological + self-report design was essential — it created objective validation that self-report alone couldn't provide.

**What I'd improve:** Extend beyond PSA employees; test in moving vehicles with real ambient noise to increase ecological validity.

**Open question:** Does the relaxation effect habituate with repeated exposure, or does consistency maintain its impact over time?

## SLIDE 9 — Appendix: Methods Detail

**Statistical analysis:**
- Descriptive statistics per condition
- PCA to identify latent factor structure in self-report
- Multiple regression: predictors of relaxation and arousal

**Instruments:**
- EDA: continuous skin conductance (µS)
- HR: continuous BPM
- Mood/arousal questionnaire (standard psychometric scales)
- Bartneck scale (2009) for "living characteristics" perception
