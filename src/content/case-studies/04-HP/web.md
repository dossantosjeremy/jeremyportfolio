# When Financing Is the Surprise, Not the Plan: Reframing HP's eCommerce Buying Journey

---

## The Context

HP.com US is one of HP's primary direct sales channels — printers, laptops, desktops, accessories, for both consumers and SMBs. The eCommerce team had offered financing for years (monthly payment plans through third-party providers), but it was buried in the checkout flow and almost never factored into purchase decisions.

I joined as Lead UX Researcher and, over two-plus years, ran eight research projects covering financing, subscriptions, promotions, login, cross-sell behaviour, returns, and bundle strategy. The financing discovery project was the first and most consequential — it reshaped how HP thought about the feature entirely.

Each project I ran was also intended to build a repeatable research foundation for the team. Before I started, there was no structured research programme on the eCommerce side. By the time I left, there was a prioritised research backlog, a consistent methodology, and a team that trusted research to drive roadmap decisions.

---

## The Problem

HP's financing options were available but commercially invisible. Customers discovered them by accident — usually at checkout, after the purchase decision was already made. The team's assumption was that if they surfaced financing earlier, more customers would factor it into their decision and conversion would improve.

The real question: when in the journey does financing actually matter to customers, and what job are they using it to do?

There was also no clear picture of how HP's financing experience compared to competitors. The feature had grown organically without a defined UX strategy.

---

## What I Did

### Financing Discovery

I started with competitive benchmarking — analysed 8+ eCommerce players (Amazon, Dell, Best Buy, Apple, and others) to understand how financing was positioned and where it appeared in the journey. This gave the team a shared frame before any user research ran.

Then unmoderated user testing via UserTesting. I recruited participants across consumer and SMB segments, US market. The goal: follow real purchase journeys on HP.com and identify where and how financing registered.

I also ran stakeholder interviews across product, design, finance, and marketing — because financing isn't just a UX problem. The feature sat at the intersection of four teams who had different assumptions about how it worked and who it was for.

### Downstream Studies

Over the following two years I ran seven additional projects on HP.com:

- **Subscriptions discovery** — understanding how customers thought about print subscriptions vs. one-off purchases, and what triggered subscription consideration
- **Promotions research (India)** — how Indian customers interpreted and responded to promotional messaging, which differed significantly from US patterns
- **Login experience (India)** — friction in the HP account login flow, with specific focus on the Indian market context
- **Cross-sell behaviour** — when and how customers responded to accessory and consumable recommendations
- **Bundle strategy** — how customers evaluated pre-configured bundles vs. individual component selection
- **Printer choice (Mexico)** — how customers navigated the printer product page and made model selection decisions
- **Returns (UK)** — evaluating the post-sales return flow for defective and unwanted products

---

## Key Findings

**The financing insight that changed the strategy:**

Financing wasn't a decision driver. It was a delight trigger.

Customers who were already committed to buying the product — who had made up their minds — discovered financing at checkout and reacted positively. The feeling was: *"I can get this now, and the monthly payment makes it feel manageable."* It reinforced a decision already made. It didn't make the decision.

This reframed the entire product strategy. The team had been planning to push financing higher in the funnel to influence more decisions. The research said that was the wrong goal. The right goal: make financing discoverable for customers who are close to buying, not for customers who are still comparing options.

**From the competitive analysis:** Competitors who surfaced financing too early (on category pages, in navigation) created noise rather than conversion. The ones who did it well tied it to specific product pages and high-intent moments.

**From the returns study (UK):** Significant confusion between defective product returns and unwanted product returns. Customers couldn't identify which flow applied to them. The visual distinction between the two paths wasn't clear enough in the existing UI.

**From promotions (India):** Indian customers required explicit promotions framing. Implicit discounts (e.g., a price reduction without a badge or label) weren't read as promotions at all. This was a consistent finding across sessions that surprised the central team.

---

## The Impact

- Financing product strategy reframed from "push it earlier" to "make it visible at high-intent moments" — directly informing the roadmap
- E2E financing journey architecture defined, used as the foundation for design and engineering work
- Competitive benchmarking used as a reference by the design team for 12+ months
- Returns flow issues documented and prioritised by the UK product team
- India-specific findings (promotions + login) fed into a dedicated India market research programme
- Eight projects delivered over two years, creating HP's first structured eCommerce research programme on the US team

---

## What I Learned

Managing eight concurrent or sequential research projects across product, design, finance, and marketing required more stakeholder coordination than I expected going in. Each study had different sponsors with different assumptions about what the research would find. Getting alignment on the research question before each study — not just at the end — was the part that made the work land.

The India market findings were the most instructive. Running studies in the US and assuming the patterns generalised was the default assumption on the team. The India work showed clearly that customer behaviour, mental models around promotions, and technology interaction patterns were different enough to need their own research track. That insight was harder to act on politically than it was to generate.

The thing I'd do differently: I'd have pushed for a shared synthesis artefact earlier — something that connected the findings across all eight studies into a single customer model. Each study was acted on individually. The cross-study patterns were visible to me but harder to communicate without a dedicated synthesis output.
