# Evaluating HP's New Financing Experience End-to-End

> **One-line hook**: Discovery research told us *what* users needed from financing. This study validated whether a newly designed flow actually delivered it — across 12 participants, testing for convenience, transparency, and decision confidence.

## Background

From the preceding Financing Discovery Research, we knew that financing at HP was a "delight trigger" — discovered late in the journey, not part of the original purchase decision. The discovery study had also surfaced what users needed: convenience, fast pre-qualification, transparent communication, and clear terms near the product.

The Financing Evaluation Research had one job: **validate whether the newly designed financing flow delivered those qualities.**

I led the study end-to-end and supervised a second UX researcher during delivery.

## Research Questions

1. What are users' first impressions and awareness levels of the prototype?
2. How intuitive is the current financing experience?
3. What are users' attitudes, and how does the experience align with their expectations?

**Hypotheses tested:**
- Clear, accessible language about financing terms increases understanding and confidence
- Emphasising 0% interest, short duration, and the ease of the process increases confidence
- Frequent, transparent communication throughout the flow reduces friction in decision-making
- Showing financing information in the user's profile increases trust in HP

## Methodology

**12 unmoderated UserTesting sessions**, structured in three phases:

![Test plan — three-phase structure: contextual dive-in, usability task, and follow-up questions.](images/hp-financing-eval-test-plan.png)
*Test plan — three-phase structure: contextual dive-in, usability task, and follow-up questions.*
- Contextual dive-in (first impressions, awareness)
- Usability test (task completion, navigation)
- Follow-up questions (attitudes, expectations)

**Participants screened into two segments:**
- *Past users* — bought an electronic device with financing in the last 6 months
- *Active researchers* — currently researching electronics, open to financing but no prior experience

Age range: 25–34, 35–44, 45–54, 55–64.

## Key Insights

**1. Above-the-fold placement is critical.**

![User behaviour analysis from UserTesting — mapping where participants engaged and missed financing information.](images/hp-financing-eval-behavior-analysis.png)
*User behaviour analysis from UserTesting — mapping where participants engaged and missed financing information.* Participants only engaged with financing elements when they appeared above the scroll line. Information placed lower was systematically missed.

> *"I would expect where it says how does financing work to be at the top because in the homepage where I started, I wanted to view more..."*

**2. The prototype was rated intuitive — but layout caused findability issues.** Navigation and content clarity scored well. The structural issue was where specific information appeared in the page hierarchy.

> *"Experience was user-friendly, intuitive. Navigation was great. No need to change anything. I felt that was very easy."*

**3. A dedicated financing landing page was highly valued.** Participants appreciated having all information in one place: plans comparison, business vs. consumer division, eligibility checking. One page for everything matched their mental model.

---

## Recommendations

1. **Move the highest-impact information above the fold** — "Spread costs over time, up to N months" should be visible without scrolling
2. **Show eligibility checking as early as possible** — it's a key factor in decision-making and users want it surfaced pre-cart
3. **Keep the process to the minimum number of steps** — complexity erodes confidence
4. **Make terms and conditions explicit** — interest rates, payment duration — so users can make informed decisions
5. **Reorder the financing landing page** — put "How financing works" at the top, matching the user's first question
