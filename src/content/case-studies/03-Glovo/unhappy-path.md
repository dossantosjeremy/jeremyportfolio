# Designing for the Unhappy Path: When Couriers Don't Get Orders

> **One-line hook**: "I get nervous because I have to pay for a lot of things, I have two children, I have to feed the family." — When couriers sit idle waiting for orders, Glovo had no answer. I researched what to communicate and when to reduce their anxiety — and found that Glovo was completely out of the loop while couriers had already built their own troubleshooting systems.

## Background & Problem

The Delivery Solutions team wanted to explore what Glovo should communicate to couriers when no orders can be assigned — to reduce anxiety, reduce contacts to human support, and improve courier experience.

**My starting point was an audit of support chat logs.** The patterns were revealing: couriers contacted Glovo not because they didn't know what to do — but because the app gave them nothing. They had developed their own informal troubleshooting routines (checking battery, GPS, internet connection, going to the city centre) but had no confirmation that these steps actually worked. They were operating in the dark, and their anxiety came from that lack of visibility — not the waiting itself.

![Current in-app support experience when not receiving orders](images/glovo-unhappy-path-app-support-screen.png)
*Current experience: the in-app contact tree when couriers can't get orders — no guidance, no troubleshooting, no feedback loop.*

**A key finding from the audit:** Couriers were actively avoiding the in-app bot. Support agents could not diagnose the root cause of the problem. There was no self-service path for a technical issue with real financial stakes.

![Chat log audit excerpt](images/glovo-unhappy-path-chat-audit.png)

## Research Approach

**Competitive analysis** — I mapped how direct and indirect competitors handled the "no orders" state. Most offered: a list of troubleshooting steps; an active "searching for orders" indicator; a clear "no orders available right now" message. None of Glovo's competitors left couriers in silence.

**Research hypotheses:**
1. Displaying how couriers can get more orders — at the moment they contact support or even before — can reduce anxiety
2. A self-service tool to help couriers refresh the system automatically will decrease anxiety more than a human agent

**9 moderated interviews, 1 hour each** — Couriers from Spain, Italy, and Romania, selected because these markets generated the highest volume of "no orders" support contacts. Participants included both new and experienced couriers, those who had waited with no orders before, and those who had experience with both bot and human agents.

## Key Insights

**Two primary patterns emerged from affinity diagramming:**

![Affinity diagram from Miro](images/glovo-unhappy-path-affinity-diagram.png)
*Affinity diagram built in Miro — interview data clustered across themes: knowledge of order assignment, reasons it happens, mental models for addressing it, feelings, and expectations.*

![Insight extraction map](images/glovo-unhappy-path-insight-extraction.png)
*Secondary insight extraction — mapping the connections between what couriers know, what triggers non-receipt, and how they mentally model resolution.*

**1. Couriers lack visibility on the origin of the problem — and have no single source of truth.**

> *"I use it to speak with an agent so he can check if there's an order 'stuck' or not"* — a courier in Italy
>
> *"I look at my battery and that it is charged, that nothing is wrong, that I am online and that I am somewhere in the city centre"* — a courier in Romania

Couriers had developed personal checklists and troubleshooting rituals — but these were informal and idiosyncratic. Some checked connectivity; others drove to busier areas. None of this was surfaced, guided, or acknowledged by the app.

**2. With experience, couriers develop coping strategies — but Glovo is out of the loop.**

Experienced couriers had effective mental models for diagnosing the problem. But this knowledge wasn't shared, wasn't encouraged, and wasn't visible to support agents. Every contact with support started from zero.

![Courier journey map](images/glovo-unhappy-path-journey-map.png)
*Courier journey map across 4 phases: Waiting for an order → Considering contact → Contact → Resolution. Anxiety peaks in the absence of visibility; resolution is undermined by agents lacking the same tools couriers use.*

---

## UX Recommendations

1. **Create a single source of truth** — a resource inside the app that explains what is happening (system issue vs. low demand vs. location problem) and provides structured troubleshooting steps. This mirrors what the best-performing competitors already offer.

2. **Provide couriers and agents with shared tools** — so that when a courier does contact support, the agent can see the troubleshooting steps the courier has already taken, and pick up from there rather than starting from scratch.

3. **Surface the troubleshooting knowledge that experienced couriers have already developed** — through in-app guidance, contextual tips, or peer-sourced content.

**Key learning:** There is always room for UX research even when the root problem is technical. The issue was not the technology — it was the communication gap and the absence of a feedback loop. Framing the research around the *user experience of uncertainty* rather than the *technical failure* unlocked a more actionable set of findings.
