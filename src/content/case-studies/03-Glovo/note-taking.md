# Improving the Note-Taking Experience (Research Ops)

> **One-line hook**: Great research can be undermined by poor note-taking. At Glovo, UX Research ran dozens of studies with cross-functional observers — but no standardised process for how stakeholders should take notes. This internal research project changed that.

## Background & Problem

This project was part of the UX Team's internal OKR initiative on improving collaboration with stakeholders and the actionability of research insights.

The team observed that when stakeholders joined research sessions as observers and note-takers, the quality of notes varied significantly — making synthesis harder, slowing down debrief sessions, and reducing the value of having stakeholders in the room at all.

![The note-taking context: Glovo UX Research sessions with cross-functional observers. Inconsistent notes → slower synthesis → less actionable insights.](images/glovo-note-taking-context.jpg)
*The note-taking context: Glovo UX Research sessions with cross-functional observers. Inconsistent notes → slower synthesis → less actionable insights.*

**Research questions:**
- How might we improve the note-taking process at every step of a research session?
- How might we educate stakeholders on best practices for note-taking during user research?

---

## Research Approach

**5 semi-directive interviews (30 minutes each)** with stakeholders who regularly joined research sessions as observers. Participants included Product Designers, Product Managers, and Product Analysts.

**Affinity diagramming** to cluster themes across interviews.

**Persona definition** to segment note-taker types and tailor recommendations accordingly.

![Participant clustering from interview data — grouping 5 stakeholders into distinct note-taker profiles.](images/glovo-note-taking-persona-clusters.png)
*Participant clustering from interview data — grouping 5 stakeholders into distinct note-taker profiles.*

---

## Key Insights

Three findings emerged from synthesis:

1. **Making notes understandable by others is time-consuming.** Stakeholders were taking notes for themselves, not for the research team — creating a translation layer that didn't exist.

2. **Taking notes takes attention away from participants.** Trying to transcribe and observe simultaneously degraded the quality of both.

3. **Note-takers don't know in advance what the UX Researcher needs.** Without a briefing or template, stakeholders defaulted to capturing what they personally found interesting — which didn't always align with research objectives.

**Two personas defined:**
- *The Engaged Observer* — motivated but unsure of what to capture; needs a template and pre-session briefing
- *The Passive Attendee* — joins for visibility, low engagement; needs clearer role definition and structured prompts

## Recommendations & Deliverables

1. **Schedule post-session debriefs** — structured 15-minute sessions immediately after each study to consolidate notes while memory is fresh
2. **Researcher briefing before sessions** — communicate objectives and note-taking focus areas before each study
3. **Develop a note-taking template** — a structured baseline capturing: participant quote, observation, and interpretation as separate fields

**What was built:**
- A note-taking template deployed as a Miro/Google Sheets baseline for all future studies
- A Confluence documentation page with process, guidelines, dos and don'ts for observer note-takers
- Two personas to guide future training and onboarding of stakeholder observers

## Impact

The standardised note-taking process was adopted across the UX team. A follow-up tree test assessed the impact of the Confluence documentation on stakeholder comprehension.

**Key learning:** ResearchOps work is invisible until it breaks.
