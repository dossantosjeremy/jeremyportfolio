# Rebuilding Trust with 40,000 Couriers: How Research Redesigned the Glovo Support Experience

---

## The Context

Glovo operates across 25+ countries. Couriers — independent contractors — use the app to manage deliveries, report issues, and access earnings. The Delivery Solutions team owned the support experience inside the courier app, with a primary KPI: reduce the contact ratio. Every issue a courier resolved without calling an agent saved the company money and saved the courier time.

The support section (called the contact tree) was the self-service channel. It wasn't working. Couriers were bypassing it to reach a human agent instead.

I joined the team on this as my first project — launched in my first month at Glovo. Three sequential research projects over two years, each building on the last.

---

## The Problem

Couriers weren't selecting the wrong options because they were confused. They were selecting whatever got them to an agent fastest.

The contact tree was built around Glovo's internal taxonomy — not how couriers thought about their own problems. A courier whose order was cancelled mid-delivery didn't think "Delivery Issues > Order Management > Cancellation." They thought "my order was cancelled." The structure made sense internally. It made no sense to the people using it.

> *"I would prefer if we had to click only on a few buttons and not have to click 2 or 3 times."* — Courier, Morocco

The business goal was clear: redesign the support structure so couriers could actually resolve issues themselves. Every percentage point drop in the contact ratio had direct cost impact.

---

## What I Did

### Project 1 — Courier Support App Redesign (March 2022)

I started with a heuristic analysis of the existing contact tree before speaking to any couriers. That gave me a baseline: too many options on the first level, no icons for couriers who weren't fluent in the app's language, and critical options buried next to high-frequency ones.

Then card sorting. I needed to understand how couriers categorised their own problems — not how Glovo did. Card sorting externalises mental models without leading participants toward a pre-existing structure. I ran sessions across Spain, Portugal, and Morocco, remotely, in multiple languages.

The result was clear: couriers categorise by three things — frequency (how often does this happen), severity (how urgent is it), and delivery phase (pick-up, drop-off, or earnings). Glovo's IA ignored delivery phase entirely.

I also ran usability tests on a redesign prototype that the team was developing in parallel. I embedded this into the courier sessions so I could get both discovery data and design feedback in a single pass.

### Project 2 — Earnings Education Validation (June 2022)

Earnings calculation was a major source of courier anxiety. Couriers frequently submitted support tickets challenging their pay — often without understanding how it was calculated. The product team built a prototype to make earnings transparent and let couriers submit adjustment requests directly.

My job: validate it before launch.

I used moderated usability testing via Zoom with a Figma prototype, plus unmoderated testing via Maze to reach couriers across markets without booking individual sessions.

Key findings: the prototype made earnings significantly easier to understand. Couriers felt it was fairer. But the estimator was hard to find, the adjustment request flow wasn't intuitive, and the copy was over-explanatory in a way that felt patronising.

The unexpected outcome: the research elevated earnings transparency from a support feature (buried in the contact tree) to a standalone product feature. The Fintech team adopted the concept. Earnings became its own product area.

### Project 3 — IA Tree Test (January 2023)

The redesigned contact tree was ready to roll out. Before launch, we needed to know: does it actually work?

I designed an unmoderated tree test using UserZoom. 284 couriers across markets, 6 tasks per session covering the most common support needs. Compared the redesigned IA against the existing one on findability, directness, and time to complete.

I chose unmoderated specifically because of the tight pre-launch timeline. Moderated sessions would have taken three weeks to recruit and run. Unmoderated at this scale got us the statistical confidence we needed inside a week.

---

## Key Findings

**From card sorting:** Couriers use delivery phase — not issue type — as their primary mental model for support. This was the single most important structural insight and the basis for the redesign.

**From usability testing:** The redesign significantly improved couriers' ability to navigate. Icons helped. The column layout worked. Couriers noticed and appreciated both.

**From tree testing:**

| Metric | Current IA | Redesigned IA |
|---|---|---|
| Average findability | Baseline | +58% |
| Support escalation rate | Baseline | −23% post-launch |
| Task directness | Baseline | Significantly improved |

Sample: 284–300+ participants. The redesigned IA was adopted as the standard across Spain, Portugal, and Morocco.

**The earnings finding** was the one nobody expected. The research didn't just improve the support section — it removed a whole category of support contact by turning it into a product feature.

---

## The Impact

- **+58% findability** in tree test validation (284+ couriers)
- **−23% support escalation** following IA redesign rollout
- IA redesign adopted as company standard across three markets
- Earnings feature elevated to Fintech product — removed from support entirely
- Established the first courier research programme at Glovo, creating a repeatable foundation for future studies

---

## What I Learned

Running three connected studies over two years meant each one was better for what I learned in the previous one. The tree test was significantly sharper because I already knew the mental models from card sorting. That sequencing was intentional — but it also meant I had to sell each follow-on study before the previous one was finished, which required keeping stakeholders aligned throughout.

The thing I'd do differently: I would have pushed harder to get external couriers into the first study. Working within PSA's internal pool (Glovo employees who knew the app well) compressed the recruitment timeline but limited what we could learn about first-time or infrequent support users.

The earnings finding was a good reminder that research questions should stay open longer than the scope suggests. The brief was about the contact tree. But listening to couriers broadly enough to catch the earnings anxiety — and recognising its significance — was what created the most value from the programme.
